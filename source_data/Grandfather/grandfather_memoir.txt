I was born in a small village nestled in the rolling hills of the Punjab exactly eighty years ago, in the year 1926. In those days, we were subjects of the British Crown, a reality that cast a long shadow over our daily lives.

I still vividly recall the salt tax and the protests that rippled through our communities when I was just a boy. Though we were poor farmers working the land, we felt the heavy hand of the Raj in the taxes collected from our meager harvests. My own father was disciplined by the local authorities simply for organizing a peaceful gathering in our village square to discuss self-reliance. 

We grew our own wheat and sugarcane, waking before dawn to tend the fields. Life was incredibly hard but deeply communal. Every evening, the village elders would gather under the great banyan tree to share stories and dream of a free India—a dream that finally came true in 1947, though the partition that followed tore many families apart. 

I have seen the world change from bullock carts to motorcars, from letters that took weeks to arrive to this age where information travels through the air invisible to the eye. But I always tell my grandchildren: never forget the soil from which you came, for a man without roots is like a leaf blown aimlessly by the wind. They tell me to rest now, but a farmer never truly stops working in his heart.
